### UI Aesthetic Direction (added 2026-05-23 — Knowji teardown)

**Reference:** Notion / Superhuman — sleek, minimalist, premium productivity tool. NOT a children’s game.

The vocabulary app market is saturated with brightly colored, cartoonish interfaces targeting children and casual learners. Knowji’s “cheesy and exaggerated” cartoon illustrations are its primary differentiator for visual learners — and its primary liability with adult professionals, who find the aesthetic juvenile and alienating. This is a documented, research-confirmed market gap.

**LexiTap’s aesthetic must signal:** serious adult learner tool. Every visual decision should pass the test: “would a professional ESL learner pull this out on their commute without embarrassment?”

**Concrete design constraints:**

- **Dark mode as primary** — not a toggle afterthought. Dark-mode-first signals premium and reduces eye strain during commute study
- **Typography-driven layouts** — clean type hierarchy, generous whitespace, minimal decorative elements
- **No cartoon characters or illustrated mascots** — Knowji owns that lane; LexiTap explicitly does not enter it
- **No bright primary-color overload** — the teal primary is clean; supporting UI should not feel like a classroom poster
- **Imagery is contextual, not decorative** — images (ImageMatch widget, word context) serve the learning function; they are not whimsical ornaments
- **Micro-animations: subtle, functional** — confirm interactions, not celebrate them; haptic feedback confirms taps without fanfare

## Marketing Pillars

*Top-level claims that should anchor landing page, App Store listing, and core marketing copy. Each is true today (no future-promising), defensible against competitor counter-claims, and tied to a documented competitor weakness or industry pattern (see Competitive Analysis doc).*

**1. Own forever. No auto-renewal. No subscription traps.**

- Tier purchases are one-time. Premium Pass is annual but never silently auto-renews into another cycle without explicit consent.
- Direct contrast to ELSA Speak's documented auto-renewal backlash and the broader subscription-fatigue trend.
- Sample copy: *"Pay once per pack. Own it forever. No surprise renewals."*

**2. Zero ads. Ever.**

- No interstitials. No banner ads. No rewarded video. No "upgrade to remove ads" upsell.
- Direct contrast to **Duolingo's ad-supported freemium model** (their Super tier at ~$84/yr exists primarily to remove ads). LexiTap has nothing to remove.
- Sample copy: *"No ads in the free tier. No ads in the paid tiers. No ad-removal upsell, because there's nothing to remove."*

**3. Best bang for the buck in English vocab.**

- Premium Pass at $29.99/yr unlocks all paid tiers. Industry comparison:
    - Babbel: ~$168/yr
    - Knowji: ~$120/yr
    - Memrise: ~$90/yr
    - ELSA Speak: ~$89-99/yr
    - Duolingo Super: ~$84/yr
- LexiTap delivers comparable vocab-mastery surface at roughly one-third the price of the cheapest comparable competitor.
- Sample copy: *"Everything for $29.99/year. Or pay per pack from $2.99. The other apps charge that monthly."*

**4. No typing. Mobile-first. Offline-first.**

- Existing core UX claim. Retained. Anchor of the product name itself.

**5. Designed for non-native English speakers serious about real-world fluency.**

- Not for casual learners (Duolingo's lane).
- Not for native speakers expanding lexicon (WordUp partial overlap; [Vocabulary.com](http://Vocabulary.com)'s lane).
- Not for K-12 / SAT prep (the separate American-student app's lane per audience-split decision).
- The serious-non-native focus is LexiTap's tighter positioning vs. WordUp's broader scope.
- Sample copy: *"For ESL learners with a goal: pass the test, get the job, work the room."*

## Brand Guidelines

**DO:**

- Emphasize "no typing" in all marketing
- Lean on the four Marketing Pillars above (own-forever, zero-ads, best-bang-for-the-buck, no-typing/mobile/offline)
- Use tap/gesture imagery
- Keep messaging simple and clear
- Focus on mobile-first experience
- Highlight free cloud sync

**DON'T:**

- Use stock photos of people typing on keyboards
- Compare directly to Duolingo (different audience — see Competitive Frame in Product Strategy Overview)
- Position against Knowji on price alone (their direct value-prop overlap is the substantive concern; price is the visible symptom)
- Over-promise ("fluent in 30 days")
- Use complex academic jargon
- Show ads or tracking (privacy promise)
- Commit to specific vocabulary counts in copy
- **Use cartoon illustrations or character-based imagery** — Knowji’s lane; their “cheesy and exaggerated” cartoon aesthetic is a documented liability with adult professionals, which is exactly LexiTap’s audience. Entering this aesthetic signals “children’s app” to the user LexiTap is trying to win.
- **Use bright, primary-color-overloaded UI** — the adult-professional aesthetic (Notion / Superhuman reference) is the competitive differentiator; visual clutter undercuts it ("10,000+ words", "800 words") — content sourcing determines actuals; tier names and CEFR levels are the durable claims
